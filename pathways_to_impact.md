# Pathways to Impact: Executive Summary (DRAFT)

## Introduction

Researchers are working in a rapidly changing academic environment. Expectations around the role of research in society are evolving, and new forms of engagement are emerging across disciplines and sectors.

These changes extend beyond demonstrating outcomes. They reflect broader shifts in how research is valued, supported, and connected to society, including:

- A growing emphasis on research impact, shaped by urgent societal challenges and renewed attention to public trust in research
- Changing expectations from funders and governments around the public value of research
- A shift toward more inclusive, responsible, and meaningful approaches to research assessment

In this context, researchers are increasingly asked to think about impact as part of an ongoing process that unfolds across the life of a research project. For research to lead to meaningful impact, certain practices, relationships, and forms of support need to be in place over time.

### Defining Research Impact

At Concordia, we define research impact as the change that research activities contribute to society and beyond. We understand this change as an ongoing process of engagement that can lead to academic, social, cultural, economic, environmental, or policy effects. The consequences of research, or impacts, can:

- be positive or negative
- be of different types or natures
- occur at different times and scales
- result from the research process itself, as well as from its results

### Framing Research Impact

We consider the impact areas below as a way of framing the different forms research impact can take. These areas do not capture the full range of how impact is understood or experienced but offer a starting point to help clarify research impact goals and advance them with greater intention, capacity and assessment.

- **Academic Impact:** Contributing to knowledge development and creation via publications and knowledge mobilization activities such as datasets, open access outputs, or conference presentations.
- **Cultural Impact:** Relating to heritage, the arts, or sociological elements such as norms, values, ideas, and attitudinal changes.
- **Economic Impact:** Driving economic development, generating new products and services, creating or contributing to enterprises.
- **Environmental Impact:** Benefiting natural and built environments, ecosystems, and associated communities.
- **Health and Wellbeing Impact:** Advancing drugs, treatments, public awareness, education, training, and access to healthcare.
- **Policy Impact:** Informing or influencing decision-making by governments, public bodies, NGOs, or the private sector.
- **Social Impact:** Informing public debate, improving welfare, equality, inclusion, quality of life, and opportunities.

This understanding of impact underpins the need for flexible approaches that can respond to different research contexts, goals, and moments in the research process.

### Concordia’s Support for Researchers

At Concordia University, we aim to support researchers by expanding and coordinating support for:

- Considering impact earlier in the research process
- Engaging more meaningfully with partners, communities, and stakeholders
- Communicating research beyond academic audiences
- Participating in open science and knowledge mobilization practices
- Exploring how research can inform action, policy, or practice
- Reflecting on how impact is understood, documented, and assessed
- Seeking appropriate institutional support and recognition for this work

## Pathways to Impact

Led by the Office of the Vice-President, Research, Innovation and Impact (OVPRII), the Pathways to Impact initiative responds to changing expectations around research impact. The initiative brings together university structures and resources to create a coordinated approach that strengthens academic, scientific, and societal impact.

At its core, the initiative is about enhancing how support is organized around researchers. By connecting researchers with expertise and services across the university and external resources, this integrated approach ensures they have access to the relationships and resources that advance their impact work.

Ultimately, we want to ensure:

- Researchers are supported to maximize the diverse impacts of their work
- Institutional units collaborate to offer tools, resources, and training that meet researcher and partner needs
- Researchers are well-positioned for success in funding
- Societal stakeholders know and value our research contributions

## The 7 Pathways

Below are the Pathways to Impact, describing ways researchers could engage to create impact and what each pathway offers.

### 1. Academic Scholarship (Scholarly Practice & Recognition)

You might engage here if you want to:

- Share your research openly and responsibly
- Communicate the significance of your work beyond publications
- Strengthen how your research is assessed and recognized
- Increase the reach and long-term value of your outputs
- Develop narrative CVs
- Plan and manage research data responsibly

### 2. Research Creation (Creative & Experimental Research)

You might engage here if you want to:

- Explore non-traditional and creative research outputs
- Use creative methods to engage audiences and address social issues
- Center experimentation, reflection, and making
- Create/test prototypes with collaborative feedback
- Integrate sustainable and ethical practices

### 3. Community Engagement (Community Partnership & Co-Creation)

You might engage here if you want to:

- Co-design research
- Build trust-based partnerships
- Share authority and decision-making
- Collaborate on research questions, methods, and outcomes
- Use participatory or citizen science approaches

### 4. Innovation (Translational Thinking)

You might engage here if you want to:

- Explore applications of your research
- Develop innovation-oriented thinking
- Test ideas before formal partnerships
- Reframe problems and opportunities

### 5. Entrepreneurship & Commercialization

You might engage here if you want to:

- Develop industry partnerships
- Explore entrepreneurial pathways
- Protect and manage IP
- License or commercialize research
- Navigate agreements and commercialization routes

### 6. Public Policy (Policy Advocacy & Systems Influence)

You might engage here if you want to:

- Work with governments, NGOs, and policy bodies
- Produce policy-tailored outputs
- Develop policy-relevant writing
- Influence curricula
- Contribute to international policy development

### 7. Communications (Public Communication & Engagement)

You might engage here if you want to:

- Reach public audiences
- Engage with media
- Contribute to public scholarship and dialogue
- Develop strategies for public-facing communication

## Concordia’s Pathways to Impact Vision

### The future we anticipate:

- Researchers are supported to maximize the diverse impacts of their work
- Multiple pathways to impact are recognized and strengthened
- Research impact is valued and visible
- Institutional units collaborate to offer needed resources and training
- Impact aligns with Concordia’s core commitments
- Achieving research impact is easy, normative, and rewarding
- Stakeholders know and value our research
- Researchers succeed in funding competitions
- Concordia is a recognized leader in research impact

Research impact, at an institutional level, is shaped by how intentionally we consider how research can benefit society. By engaging diverse pathways and creating supportive structures, the initiative strengthens the conditions for all research to generate meaningful societal benefits.
